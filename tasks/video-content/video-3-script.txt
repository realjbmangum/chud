Let's start with emergency signs. These mean you need to see a vet immediately, not tomorrow.

If your dog won't put ANY weight on the leg, that's a non-weight-bearing limp. Most dogs won't walk on a broken leg, torn ligament, or dislocated joint. If the leg is dragging on the ground, or you see an obvious broken bone or joint at an awkward angle, this is an emergency.

...

Severe swelling or deformity is another red flag. If your dog is yelping, whining, or showing signs of extreme pain, don't wait. Bleeding from the leg needs immediate attention.

And here's critical: if limping is accompanied by lethargy, disorientation, vomiting, or breathing difficulty, these could signal serious systemic problems. Go to the emergency vet.

...

What should you do? Call the emergency vet immediately. For transport, support small dogs' heads and hips. For larger dogs unable to walk, use a blanket as a sling to carry them. Don't force them to walk if they're in severe pain.

...

Now let's talk about wait-and-see signs. These mean you can monitor at home for twenty-four hours before calling your regular vet.

If your dog still puts SOME weight on the leg, that's weight-bearing. It's a mild limp with no other symptoms. No visible injury, swelling, or major deformity. Your dog is still eating, drinking, and acting relatively normal. The limp had gradual onset, not sudden.

...

Important rule: if the lameness persists for more than twenty-four hours, you need to seek veterinary care even if it seems minor. Twenty-four hours is your guideline, not a week.

...

During that twenty-four hour monitoring period, confine your dog. Restrict activity to leash-only bathroom breaks. If there's swelling, apply an ice pack for fifteen minutes. Watch for improvement. If it gets worse or doesn't improve, call your vet.

...

How do you examine your limping dog at home? First, safety. You need two people, one to examine and one to restrain. Dogs in pain may bite even people they love. If your dog is in severe pain, skip the examination and go straight to the vet.

...

Start with the paw. Check between the toes for thorns, splinters, or grass. Look for redness. Check the pads for cuts or punctures. Examine each toenail for breaks or nail bed infections.

Then work up the leg. Gently apply pressure starting at the toes, moving upward. Most dogs will pull the leg back when you touch the sore spot. Bend and flex each joint. If your dog resists, that's a sign of pain.

...

Compare to the other leg. If something looks or feels unusual, checking the unaffected leg helps you identify what's different. Look for swelling, heat, wounds, or areas of tenderness.

Here's a pro tip: record video of your dog walking. Dogs often hide limps at the vet's office, so video evidence from home helps with diagnosis.

...

Let's talk about causes briefly, so you understand what might be happening. Sudden onset limping usually means injury. Paw pad cuts, torn nails, foreign objects stuck in the paw, sprains, strains, fractures, or torn ligaments like ACL tears.

Gradual onset limping often indicates chronic conditions. Arthritis, hip dysplasia, elbow dysplasia, or patellar luxation where the kneecap slips.

...

Other causes include tick-borne diseases like Lyme, infections, abscesses, bone cancer more common in large breeds, or insect bites.

Here's the key: as a pet owner, you can't diagnose the cause. You can only decide if it's an emergency NOW or a vet visit SOON.

...

What first aid can you provide at home? If you find a foreign object in the paw, remove it if easily accessible. Use tweezers for splinters. Clean with antibacterial soap and warm water. Apply antibiotic ointment if you have it. Bandage if needed.

For mild swelling, apply an ice pack wrapped in a towel for fifteen minutes. Repeat every few hours. Do NOT use heat for the first forty-eight hours.

...

For all limping: confine your dog. Crate rest or keep them in a small room. Restrict activity to leash-only bathroom breaks. No running, jumping, stairs, or rough play. Watch for improvement over twenty-four hours.

...

What should you NEVER do? Never give human pain medications. Ibuprofen and acetaminophen are toxic to dogs. Don't give any over-the-counter medications without vet approval. Don't force your dog to walk if they're severely painful. Don't manipulate broken bones or dislocated joints. If home care isn't working and the limp doesn't improve in twenty-four hours or gets worse, see your vet.

...

Common mistakes pet owners make. Mistake one: "If my dog is still walking on it, it can't be serious." Not true. Dogs are tough and sometimes walk on painful injuries. Look at other signs like swelling, pain level, and behavior when resting.

...

Mistake two: "I should give my dog aspirin for the pain." Never give human medications without vet approval. Many are toxic. Call your vet for safe pain management options.

...

Mistake three: "Limping that comes and goes isn't serious." Actually, intermittent limping often indicates chronic conditions like arthritis or hip dysplasia. These need vet evaluation and management.

...

Mistake four: "If there's no visible injury, I don't need to worry." Many serious injuries are internal. Torn ligaments, hip dysplasia, bone cancer. You can't see these from outside. Persistent limping without visible cause still needs evaluation.

...

Mistake five: "I should wait a week to see if it gets better." Twenty-four hours is the guideline. Waiting longer delays diagnosis and can worsen outcomes.

Mild weight-bearing limp, no other symptoms, gradual onset. Examine safely at home starting with the paw, working up the leg. Provide first aid: remove foreign objects, ice for swelling, confine and rest. Never give human medications. And if limping persists past twenty-four hours, see your vet.
